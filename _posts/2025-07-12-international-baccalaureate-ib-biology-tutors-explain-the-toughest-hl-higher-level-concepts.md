<h2>ğŸ”¬ Understanding Cellular Respiration ğŸ”¬</h2>
<p>Cellular respiration is a fundamental process that converts biochemical energy from nutrients into adenosine triphosphate (ATP), releasing energy that cells use for various activities. It's a complex process, but breaking it down into simpler steps can make it more manageable. ğŸ§©</p>
<p>1. Glycolysis: This occurs in the cytoplasm and doesn't require oxygen. It breaks down glucose into pyruvate, producing a net gain of two ATP molecules and two NADH molecules.</p>
<p>2. Krebs Cycle (or Citric Acid Cycle): This occurs in the mitochondria's matrix and further breaks down pyruvate, producing more ATP, NADH, and FADH2.</p>
<p>3. Electron Transport Chain (ETC): This is the final phase of cellular respiration, where electrons from NADH and FADH2 are passed along a series of proteins to generate ATP.</p>
<h2>ğŸ”¬ Decoding Genetics: DNA Replication and Transcription ğŸ”¬</h2>
<p>Understanding genetics is crucial for success in IB Biology. DNA replication copies genetic information, while transcription generates RNA molecules from DNA templates. Here's a quick rundown:</p>
<p>1. DNA Replication: Occurs during the S phase of the cell cycle. It involves the unwinding of the double helix, the synthesis of new DNA strands, and the rejoining of the two strands.</p>
<p>2. Transcription: Occurs in the nucleus. It involves the synthesis of an mRNA molecule using the template strand of DNA, the addition of a 5' cap, and the attachment of a polyA tail.</p>
<h2>ğŸ”¬ Unraveling Evolution: Natural Selection and Genetic Drift ğŸ”¬</h2>
<p>Evolution is another critical area in IB Biology. Two main mechanisms drive evolution: natural selection and genetic drift.</p>
<p>1. Natural Selection: This nonrandom process favours traits that increase an organism's fitness to survive and reproduce in its environment.</p>
<p>2. Genetic Drift: This random process causes changes in allele frequencies within a population due to chance events, such as migration or the founder effect.</p>
