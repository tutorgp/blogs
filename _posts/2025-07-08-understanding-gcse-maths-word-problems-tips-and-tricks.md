<h2>🔑 Understanding the Question</h2>
<p>The key to solving word problems lies in understanding the question itself. Break it down into manageable parts, and identify the variables and relationships between them.</p>
<h2>🔍 Identify Key Information</h2>
<p>Identify the main quantities and relationships in the problem. For example, if a question involves a journey, focus on the distance, speed, time, and any other relevant factors.</p>
<h2>💬 Translate to Mathematical Expressions</h2>
<p>Translate the problem into mathematical expressions. For example, if a car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, the speed is 30 miles per hour (60 ÷ 2 = 30).</p>
<h3>🧭 Solving the Problem</h3>
<p>Now that you've translated the problem into mathematical expressions, it's time to solve it using the appropriate mathematical operations.</p>
<h3>📝 Practice Basic Skills</h3>
<p>Make sure you're confident with the four basic operations (+, , x, ÷) and their order of operations (BIDMAS/BODMAS). This is the foundation for solving most word problems.</p>
<h4>🧩 Break it Down</h4>
<p>Break down complex problems into smaller, more manageable parts. Solve each part separately, then combine the results to find the final answer.</p>

<h5>💡 Learning Strategies</h5>
<h5>📝 Active Learning</h5>
<p>Don't just read or listen to the explanation; try solving the problem yourself. This helps reinforce your understanding and improves your problemsolving skills.</p>
<h5>🤝 Collaborative Learning</h5>

<h5>📅 Regular Revision</h5>
<p>Revision is key to solidifying your understanding of word problems. Regularly review the concepts you've learned, and practice solving a variety of problems to maintain your skills.</p>
